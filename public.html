Hello

I’m a physicist at the University of Melbourne, and my job is to figure out what the universe is made of. Specifically, I work on dark matter — the mysterious, invisible substance that outweighs everything we can see by five to one.

What is dark matter?

When astronomers measure how fast galaxies spin, they spin far too quickly. The stars and gas we can see don’t have nearly enough gravity to hold them together — they should fly apart. And yet they don’t. Something invisible is providing the extra pull.

We call that something dark matter. It doesn’t give off light, it doesn’t absorb light, and it passes straight through ordinary matter — including you — almost without a trace. But its gravity shapes entire galaxies, bends the path of starlight, and left fingerprints on the afterglow of the Big Bang. Add it all up and dark matter makes up about 85% of all the matter in the universe. The stuff that makes up stars, planets, and people is the rare exception.

The catch: after decades of looking, we still don’t know what dark matter is. My research tries to answer that by predicting the faint signals dark matter would leave if it occasionally bumped into an ordinary atom — and by helping design the ultra-sensitive detectors, shielded deep underground, that are waiting to spot that bump. The same techniques also let us study neutrinos, the ghostly particles streaming out of the Sun and exploding stars.
